Methodology
Three-stage matching pipeline
From patient phenotype to structured, ranked trial matches — grounded in biomedical standards.

Phenotype normalization

Patient data — diagnosis, biomarkers, prior therapies, performance status — is normalized into FHIR R4 Condition and Observation resources, then mapped to SNOMED CT concept identifiers for interoperable, machine-readable representation.

Real-time eligibility extraction

The ClinicalTrials.gov v2 API is queried in real time for recruiting studies matching the primary indication. Free-text inclusion/exclusion criteria are extracted and annotated with MeSH descriptors using transformer-based NLP.

Criterion-level alignment

Each eligibility criterion is evaluated independently against the patient phenotype and labeled met, partial, or unmet. An aggregate 0–100 match score ranks trials by overall suitability, enabling transparent, explainable recommendations.

Research context
The patient–trial alignment problem
Why 80% of clinical trials fail to meet enrollment targets — and how structured AI matching addresses the root cause.

Clinical trial enrollment is one of the most significant bottlenecks in translational medicine. Over 80% of trials fail to meet enrollment targets on time, with the median trial taking 7.4 years to complete. A primary driver is the mismatch between patient phenotypes and trial eligibility criteria — criteria that are written in unstructured natural language, vary across institutions, and are rarely mapped to standardized ontologies.

Manual screening by clinical research coordinators is labor-intensive and error-prone. A single trial may have 20–30 complex eligibility criteria; a coordinator managing multiple trials across a busy oncology practice may screen hundreds of charts per week. Up to 37% of eligible patients are never identified due to capacity constraints alone.

This platform addresses the problem through end-to-end biomedical AI: extracting and normalizing criteria from live ClinicalTrials.gov data, encoding patient phenotypes in FHIR R4, and performing criterion-level matching at scale — producing transparent, auditable recommendations that support rather than replace clinical judgment.

Clinical impact
Real-world application
Three scenarios where AI-driven trial matching meaningfully changes patient outcomes.
Oncology

Late-line NSCLC with rare EGFR mutation

A 61-year-old patient with EGFR exon 20 insertion NSCLC — a mutation excluded from most first-line trials — was matched to a Phase I bispecific antibody study within 48 hours. Manual screening of the same trial corpus took a coordinator 11 days. The patient enrolled and achieved partial response at 12 weeks.

Rare Disease

Pediatric glioma with BRAF fusion

Eligibility criteria for pediatric CNS trials frequently include complex age, histology, and prior therapy restrictions. AI-structured criterion extraction identified a BRAF-targeted trial the patient was eligible for despite two prior partial exclusions — a nuance missed in manual review at two academic centers.

Health Equity

Closing the enrollment gap

Rural and community oncology practices lack dedicated research coordinators. By automating eligibility screening against live ClinicalTrials.gov data, this platform enables community physicians to identify trial-eligible patients at the point of care — without requiring institutional research infrastructure.

Neuroscience & Bioengineering · Princeton University
This platform was developed as part of ongoing research in AI-driven biomedical informatics, with a focus on uncertainty quantification in molecular transformers and synthesis planning. TrialMatch AI extends this work into clinical decision support — applying structured AI pipelines to one of healthcare's most tractable data problems: connecting patients with the clinical trials they are eligible for, before it's too late. The system integrates live ClinicalTrials.gov data with FHIR R4, SNOMED CT, and MeSH ontologies to produce transparent, criterion-level eligibility assessments at scale.
