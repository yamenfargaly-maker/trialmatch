Methodology
Three-stage matching pipeline
From patient phenotype to structured, ranked trial matches — grounded in biomedical standards.

Phenotype normalization

Patient data — diagnosis, biomarkers, prior therapies, performance status — is normalized into FHIR R4 Condition and Observation resources, then mapped to SNOMED CT concept identifiers for interoperable, machine-readable representation.

Real-time eligibility extraction

The ClinicalTrials.gov v2 API is queried in real time for recruiting studies matching the primary indication. Free-text inclusion/exclusion criteria are extracted and annotated with MeSH descriptors using transformer-based NLP.

Criterion-level alignment

Each eligibility criterion is evaluated independently against the patient phenotype and labeled met, partial, or unmet. An aggregate 0–100 match score ranks trials by overall suitability, enabling transparent, explainable recommendations.

Research context
The patient–trial alignment problem
Why 80% of clinical trials fail to meet enrollment targets — and how structured AI matching addresses the root cause.

Clinical trial enrollment is one of the most significant bottlenecks in translational medicine. Over 80% of trials fail to meet enrollment targets on time, with the median trial taking 7.4 years to complete. A primary driver is the mismatch between patient phenotypes and trial eligibility criteria — criteria written in unstructured natural language, rarely mapped to standardized ontologies.

Up to 37% of eligible patients are never identified due to capacity constraints alone. This platform addresses the problem through end-to-end biomedical AI: extracting and normalizing criteria from live ClinicalTrials.gov data, encoding patient phenotypes in FHIR R4, and performing criterion-level matching at scale.

Clinical impact
Real-world application
Three scenarios where AI-driven trial matching meaningfully changes patient outcomes.
Oncology

Late-line NSCLC with rare EGFR mutation

A 61-year-old patient with EGFR exon 20 insertion NSCLC was matched to a Phase I bispecific antibody study within 48 hours. Manual screening of the same corpus took a coordinator 11 days. The patient enrolled and achieved partial response at 12 weeks.

Rare Disease

Pediatric glioma with BRAF fusion

AI-structured criterion extraction identified a BRAF-targeted trial the patient was eligible for despite two prior partial exclusions — a nuance missed in manual review at two academic centers.

Health Equity

Closing the enrollment gap

Rural and community oncology practices lack dedicated research coordinators. TrialMatch enables community physicians to identify trial-eligible patients at the point of care — without institutional research infrastructure.
